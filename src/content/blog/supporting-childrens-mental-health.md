Children experience stress, worry, and sadness just like adults — but they often lack the vocabulary or tools to express what they are feeling. As a parent, you are in a uniquely powerful position to support your child's emotional wellbeing.

## Create a Safe Space for Feelings

Let your child know that all feelings are valid. Instead of saying "don't cry" or "there's nothing to worry about," try acknowledging their experience: "I can see you're feeling upset. I'm here with you."

This validation builds emotional security and teaches children that their inner world matters.

## Build Consistent Routines

Children thrive on predictability. Regular meal times, bedtimes, and family rituals provide a sense of safety and control — especially important during periods of change or stress.

## Limit Screen Time and Prioritise Play

Unstructured play is essential for healthy development. It builds creativity, problem-solving, and social skills. Try to carve out daily time for free play, ideally outdoors.

## Stay Connected

Children need to feel genuinely connected to their caregivers. Even brief moments of focused attention — putting down your phone, making eye contact, asking about their day — build the emotional bond that underpins resilience.

## When to Seek Help

If you notice persistent changes in your child's mood, sleep, appetite, or behaviour, it may be time to seek professional support. Signs to watch for include:

- Persistent sadness or irritability
- Withdrawal from friends or activities they used to enjoy
- Frequent physical complaints (headaches, stomach aches) without a medical cause
- Significant changes in school performance

You do not have to navigate this alone. Reaching out to a professional is a sign of strength, not weakness.
